Start here if you are new to insurance billing
A modifier is a two-digit code added to a procedure code (CPT) to give the insurance company extra information about how, where, or by whom a service was provided. Think of it as a note on your claim that says this was telehealth or this was a separate service from the other thing billed today.
Most common mistake
Many providers new to billing skip modifiers entirely or add them to every claim just in case. Both cause denials. Modifiers should only be added when the situation specifically requires one — using the wrong modifier is just as bad as missing one.
When do I need a modifier?
You need a modifier when at least one of these is true:

1. The service was via telehealth. Insurance needs to know the visit was not in person.

2. You billed two things on the same day that look related. A modifier tells the payer they were actually separate services.

3. Your payer requires one for your credential level. Some Medicaid plans require this.

4. You are billing DSMT or a group service.
Real example
A therapist does an intake (90791) and 53 minutes of psychotherapy (90837) in the same session. Without modifier 25 on the 90791, the payer bundles them and denies one. With modifier 25, both are reimbursed.
Where does the modifier go on the claim?
On a CMS-1500 claim form, modifiers go in Box 24D next to the CPT code they modify. Up to 4 modifiers per CPT code. In most billing software there is a modifier field next to each procedure line.

Same-day service modifiers
25
Significant, separately identifiable E&M service
Required when billing an intake and therapy on the same day
Goes on an evaluation code (90791) to tell the payer the intake was separately documented from the therapy also billed that day. Without it, the payer assumes both are part of the same encounter and pays only one.

Modifier 25 goes on the evaluation code — NOT on the therapy code.
Correct usage
New patient: full psychiatric eval (90791) then 53 min therapy (90837) same session. Bill: 90791-25 and 90837.
Common mistake
Do NOT add 25 to every claim. Only use when you genuinely billed two separate service types on the same date. Overuse triggers audits.

MNT billing note
MNT uses fewer modifiers than mental health billing. Most important: telehealth requires a modifier, DSMT is a separate benefit that cannot be billed on the same date as MNT, and Medicare has strict annual hour limits.
Telehealth modifiers
95
Synchronous telehealth
Standard telehealth modifier for MNT
Use when you provide MNT via live video with the patient at home. Pair with Place of Service 02.
Example
30-minute MNT reassessment over video. Patient at home. Bill: 97803 x2 units, modifier 95, POS 02.
MedicareCommercial payersPOS 02
Critical MNT notes
MNT is billed in 15-minute units
97802 and 97803 are billed per 15-minute increment. A 60-minute MNT session = 4 units. Report the number of units in the units field on your claim — not by billing the code multiple times.
Example
60-minute initial MNT: Bill 97802, 4 units. 30-minute follow-up: Bill 97803, 2 units.
MNT and DSMT are separate Medicare benefits
MNT (97802, 97803) and Diabetes Self-Management Training (G0108, G0109) are two different benefits with different annual hour limits. You cannot bill both on the same date — they will be bundled and one will be denied. DSMT requires CMS-accredited program status and a CDCES credential.
Never bill MNT + DSMT same dayDSMT requires CMS accreditation

Your modifier summary
CPT code
Current Procedural Terminology — the 5-digit numbers (like 90837) that describe what service you provided. You choose the CPT based on the type and length of your session.
ICD-10 code
The diagnosis code (like F41.1 for GAD). Insurance uses this to confirm the service was medically necessary for the patient's condition.
Modifier
A 2-digit code added to a CPT to provide extra context — like whether the service was via telehealth or was separate from another code billed that day.
Place of service
A 2-digit code telling the payer where the service occurred. 11 = office, 02 = telehealth at patient home, 10 = telehealth non-home location.
Medical necessity
The clinical justification for a service. Insurance will deny claims if documentation does not clearly show why the patient needed the service at the frequency and duration provided.
NCCI edit
National Correct Coding Initiative — a CMS policy that prevents billing two codes together when one is considered part of the other.
Prior auth (PA)
Permission from insurance before you provide a service. If required and skipped, the claim will be denied regardless of clinical appropriateness.
Bundling
When a payer pays only one code instead of two because they consider both part of the same service. Modifiers 25 and 59 can override bundling when services were truly separate.
Carve-out
When behavioral health benefits are managed by a separate company from the main medical plan. You must be enrolled with both.
CMS-1500
The standard claim form. CPT in Box 24D, ICD-10 in Box 21, modifiers in Box 24D next to the CPT, units in Box 24G.
NPI
National Provider Identifier — a unique 10-digit number assigned to every provider. Must be enrolled with each payer before billing.
Superbill
An itemized receipt with CPT codes, ICD-10 codes, provider NPI, and session date that out-of-network patients use to seek reimbursement from their insurer.
